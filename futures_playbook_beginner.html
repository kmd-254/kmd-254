Futures Trading
Complete Guide

A structured, checklist-driven playbook for beginners trading Nasdaq and S&P 500 futures (NQ, MNQ, ES, MES) on TradingView. Prioritizes survival, rule-following, and consistency over profit.

⚠ EDUCATION ONLY — Futures trading involves substantial risk of loss and is not appropriate for all investors. This playbook is for educational purposes only and does not constitute financial advice. Always practice on a simulator before risking real money. Consult a licensed financial professional before trading.
01 — Foundation

Beginner Futures Foundation

What is a futures contract?

A futures contract is a legal agreement to buy or sell something at a predetermined price on a specific future date. In index futures like ES and NQ, you are not buying stocks — you are speculating on the direction of a market index. Contracts expire quarterly (March, June, September, December). You can profit whether the market goes up (long) or down (short).


Unlike stocks, futures trade nearly 24 hours a day, 6 days a week. The Regular Trading Hours (RTH) session runs 9:30 AM – 4:00 PM ET, which is when volume and volatility are highest. The overnight "globex" session runs outside those hours with lighter volume.

How leverage works — and why it is dangerous

Futures require a margin deposit — a fraction of the full contract value. This means you control a large notional position with a small amount of capital. A 1% move in the underlying index can produce a 10× to 20× gain or loss relative to your margin. This cuts both ways equally and without mercy.


Example (MES): The MES has a notional value of roughly $26,000 at 5,200 pts. Intraday margin is approximately $40–$100 depending on your broker. A 10-point move = $50 gain or loss — on a $100 margin deposit, that is a ±50% move in your margin. Leverage is the reason most beginners lose quickly.

Why micros first? The math is simple
  • 1The same exact setup, level, and signal works identically on MES and ES. Micros are not lesser instruments — they are identical in behavior, smaller in dollar impact.
  • 2A 10-point adverse move on MES costs $50. The same move on ES costs $500. Micros let you make mistakes while you learn without destroying your account.

  • 4Discipline, consistency, and rule-following built on micros transfers directly to full-size contracts. The mindset is the same.
NQ vs ES — why Nasdaq is more volatile

The Nasdaq-100 (NQ) tracks the top 100 non-financial companies on the Nasdaq exchange — dominated by high-beta technology stocks like Apple, Nvidia, Microsoft, Amazon, and Meta. These companies react faster and harder to earnings reports, Federal Reserve announcements, and market sentiment shifts. The S&P 500 (ES) tracks 500 companies across all sectors, providing far more diversification and smoother, more predictable movement.

Step 3 — Indicators (minimum effective set)
  • 1VWAP (Volume-Weighted Average Price) — Search "VWAP" in TradingView's built-in indicators. Add to all three charts. VWAP shows the average price weighted by volume since the session opened. Price above VWAP = bullish bias. Price below VWAP = bearish bias. This is used by institutional traders daily.
  • 29 EMA and 21 EMA — Add "EMA" from built-in indicators twice on your 15-min and 5-min charts. Set one to 9 periods and one to 21 periods. The 9 crossing above the 21 signals a bullish momentum shift. 9 below 21 = bearish momentum. Use these to confirm trend direction, not as standalone entry signals.
  • 3Volume — Add the built-in Volume indicator to your 5-min chart only. Compare current candle volume to the prior two. High volume on a breakout = conviction. Low volume on a move = potential fake. Volume is your one confirmation filter.
  • 4Nothing else — No RSI, no MACD, no Bollinger Bands, no Stochastics to begin. More indicators create conflicting signals and analysis paralysis. Master these three before adding anything.
Step 4 — Key levels to draw every morning before 9:30 AM ET
  • Previous Day High (PDH) — Horizontal line at yesterday's session high. One of the most respected levels by institutional traders. A clean break above PDH can trigger continuation moves.
    Use a bright color (e.g. blue). Label it "PDH [date]".
  • Previous Day Low (PDL) — Horizontal line at yesterday's session low. A break below PDL can trigger selloffs. Bounces at PDL are common in trending markets.
    Use a contrasting color (e.g. orange).
  • Overnight High (ONH) — Highest point reached during the globex session (approx 6:00 PM – 9:30 AM ET). Often acts as resistance at the open. A break above ONH is a bullish signal.
    Use a lighter color variant to distinguish from PDH.
  • Overnight Low (ONL) — Lowest point reached overnight. Frequently tested at or near the open. Watch for rejection bounces here in bullish market conditions.
    Match color scheme to ONH.
  • Opening Range (OR) High and Low — Draw these after 9:35 or 9:45 AM ET (the first 5 or 15 minutes). The OR is the initial range the market establishes. Breaks above OR High or below OR Low are common strategy entry triggers.
    Draw after the open — can't be set in advance.
  • VWAP — Drawn automatically by the indicator. Resets each session at 9:30 AM ET. The most important intraday mean-reversion and bias reference level.
    Auto-drawn. Verify it resets at market open each day.
  • Major Support / Resistance Zones — Any obvious multi-touch price level from the past 5–10 sessions. Where price repeatedly bounced or reversed. These become magnets for price action.
    Use boxes or zones (not just lines) to mark these areas.
03 — Morning Routine

Morning Trading Routine
First 90 Minutes

Pre-market preparation (8:00 AM – 9:25 AM ET)
8:00 AM ET
Check the economic calendar
Go to Investing.com/economic-calendar or ForexFactory.com. Filter for USD high-impact events. Flag any releases before 11:00 AM. The most dangerous releases: CPI, PPI, FOMC, NFP, GDP, Fed Chair speeches. Mark the exact release time. You will not trade 2 minutes before or 5 minutes after any high-impact event.
8:30 AM ET
Draw all key levels on the 15-min chart
Open TradingView. On your 15-min MES chart, draw PDH, PDL, ONH, and ONL. Use horizontal lines. Color-code consistently (same color system every day). Note which levels are very close together — clustered levels create stronger zones.
9:00 AM ET
Determine your directional bias
Ask three questions: (1) Is price above or below VWAP right now? (2) Is price above or below PDH/PDL? (3) Has the overnight trend been up, down, or choppy? Write one sentence in your journal: "Bias is LONG if price holds above [level] / Bias is SHORT if price breaks below [level]." This forces clarity before the chaos of the open.
9:15 AM ET
Set your TradingView alerts
Set price alerts at PDH, PDL, ONH, ONL, and VWAP (see Section 07 for exact setup instructions). Name each alert clearly. Turn on sound notifications. Having alerts prevents you from staring at charts for hours — price will come to your levels.
9:25 AM ET
Final pre-open checks
Write today's daily loss limit in your journal (e.g. "I stop trading if I lose $50 today"). Confirm your simulator is connected and working. Take 3 deep breaths. Do not rush. The open is not the only chance to trade — good setups appear all morning.
The open — 9:30 to 9:45 AM ET
❌ DO NOT trade the first 1–3 candles on the 5-min chart (9:30–9:45 AM)

The first 5–15 minutes are the most chaotic window of the day. Large institutional traders are filling massive orders. Market makers are adjusting spreads. Algorithms are reacting to overnight data. Price will often spike in both directions — this is by design to trigger retail stops and create liquidity, not to provide honest signal.


During this window: watch, observe, take notes. Do not place orders. Your job is to see which side of PDH/PDL/VWAP price is gravitating toward. Let the dust settle.

04 — Strategy

Simple Strategy
From Day One

The strategy — Pullback to Key Level + VWAP confirmation

You will use one strategy only for your first 90 days. Mastery of one setup is worth more than knowledge of ten. This strategy is based on trend direction, pullbacks to meaningful price levels, and VWAP alignment. It works in both trending and mildly ranging conditions.


The core idea: identify the trend direction, wait for price to pull back to a level that makes structural sense, watch for a confirmation candle, and enter in the direction of the trend.

Strategy flow — the 5 steps
1

2
Confirm VWAP alignment
3

4
Confirmation candle forms
5

Setup requirements — ALL five must be true before entering
  • Trend is clear on the 15-min chart — You see a series of higher highs and higher lows (uptrend) or lower highs and lower lows (downtrend). If you cannot clearly identify it in 10 seconds, the market is choppy. Skip it.
    CRITICAL — No trend = no trade
  • Price is on the correct side of VWAP — For long trades: price must be above VWAP at setup time. For short trades: price must be below VWAP. VWAP crossing is a warning sign, not a setup.
    CRITICAL — VWAP alignment is non-negotiable
  • Price has pulled back to a meaningful level — PDH, PDL, ONH, ONL, OR high/low, VWAP, or a prior swing high/low that has been tested before. "Meaningful" means price has reacted to it at least once in the recent past.
    CRITICAL — Random levels produce random results
  • A confirmation candle has formed on the 5-min chart — A bullish engulfing candle, hammer, or pin bar with a rejection wick pointing away from the level. The candle must be fully closed before you enter. Never enter on an open, incomplete candle.
    CRITICAL — Wait for the close, every time
  • Volume is equal to or higher than the prior two candles — The confirmation candle should show participation. A weak, low-volume candle is less reliable. This is a supporting filter, not a dealbreaker by itself.
    Supporting confirmation — increases probability

05 — Pre-Trade Checklist

Checklist Before
Every Single Trade

Rules for this checklist

Run this checklist before every trade — sim or live. Items marked CRITICAL require a YES answer. If any critical item is NO, the trade is skipped — no exceptions, no arguing with yourself, no "close enough." Print this section and keep it beside your keyboard.

Market context — verify the environment
  • Is price near a key level? (PDH, PDL, ONH, ONL, VWAP, Opening Range, prior swing) CRITICAL
    No random entries. Price must be at a meaningful level with a logical reason to react.
  • Is the trend clear on the 15-min chart? CRITICAL
    Higher highs/lows (uptrend) or lower highs/lows (downtrend). Tangled EMAs = no trade.
  • Is VWAP aligned with my trade direction? CRITICAL
    Long above VWAP only. Short below VWAP only. No exceptions.
Risk management — verify before entering
  • Is risk/reward at least 1:1, ideally 1:2? CRITICAL
    Calculate before entering: (target distance) ÷ (stop distance) must be ≥1.0. Below 1.0 = skip it.
  • Is my stop placed at a logical location? (behind a level or structure, not an arbitrary number) CRITICAL
    Stop must have a reason. "I don't want to lose more than $X" is not a stop placement strategy.
  • Is my position size correct for this stop distance?
    Risk amount ÷ (stop in ticks × tick value) = max contracts. Do the math before entering.
Execution quality — check your behavior
  • Am I NOT chasing price? (Price is still near the setup level, not far away) CRITICAL
    If price has moved more than 50% toward your target already, the entry is late. Skip it.
  • Did I wait for the confirmation candle to fully close? CRITICAL
    Entering on an open (incomplete) candle is anticipating, not confirming. Always wait for close.
  • Is volume supporting the setup? (Equal to or higher than prior 2 candles)
    Supporting condition — low volume alone is not a dealbreaker, but zero volume is a warning.
External conditions — verify environment is safe
  • Is this outside a high-impact news window? (No major release within 5 minutes before or after) CRITICAL
    Check the calendar before every trade. News events destroy setups instantly.
  • Have I NOT reached my daily loss limit or trade count? CRITICAL
    Write the limit before trading. When hit, close the platform. No negotiations with yourself.
  • Am I calm — not emotional, frustrated, euphoric, or revenge-trading? CRITICAL
    Emotional state is as important as market structure. Angry traders make bad decisions.
  • Is this within the preferred trading window? (9:45 AM – 11:30 AM or 1:30 PM – 3:00 PM ET)
    Softer requirement — but midday and end-of-day are notably harder for beginners.

06 — Prop Firm Rules

Prop Firm
Passing Rules

Understanding prop firm challenges

Prop firms provide funded trading capital in exchange for a share of profits. To access their capital, you must first pass an "evaluation" or "challenge" — trading a demo account to prove consistent, rule-following profitability. Most challenges run for 10–30 trading days. Common firms include Apex, Topstep, TradeDay, Earn2Trade, and others. Rules vary significantly — always read the exact terms of your specific challenge.

The prop firm mindset — 8 non-negotiable rules
  • 01Stop after 2 consecutive losses. Log off. Walk away. Review what went wrong. The fastest way to fail a challenge is revenge-trading after two losses. The market will still be there tomorrow.
  • 02Stop after hitting your daily profit target. One of the most common reasons challenges fail is over-trading on a good day. When you've made your target, take the win and close the platform.
  • 03Never widen or move a stop loss against your trade. The stop exists to protect you. Moving it away from price when losing is not "giving it room" — it is increasing your loss. This behavior alone fails more challenges than any other single mistake.
  • 04Never hold a losing position hoping it recovers. If your thesis is broken (price broke the key level, VWAP crossed, structure changed), exit immediately. Hope is not a trading strategy.
  • 05Never hold trades through major news events. FOMC, CPI, NFP, GDP, Fed speeches — exit before these or do not enter near them. News-driven price movement is frequently counter-intuitive and can trigger your stop in milliseconds.
  • 06Trade small in the first half of the challenge. Use the minimum position size for days 1–10. Scale up carefully in the second half only if the account is growing steadily. Consistency beats aggression in evaluations.
  • 07Treat every day as independent. Yesterday's big win does not make today safer. Yesterday's loss does not justify chasing today. Each day is a fresh slate with the same rules.
  • 08Know your drawdown type before trading a single contract. Some firms use trailing drawdown that follows your peak equity. If you make $1,000 profit and then give back $1,500 from peak, you may be in violation — even if you are up $500 from the start. This surprises beginners constantly.
Trailing drawdown explained — the most misunderstood prop rule

Static drawdown (simpler): Your account cannot drop more than $X below the starting balance. Once you are in profit, the floor stays at the original starting point. Example: Start at $50,000, drawdown limit $2,000 → floor is always $48,000.


Trailing drawdown (common in most firms): The floor moves up as your equity grows, but never moves down. Example: Start at $50,000 with $2,000 trailing drawdown → floor starts at $48,000. If you grow to $52,000, the floor moves to $50,000. If you then drop to $49,500, the floor is at $50,000 — you are in violation even though you are $500 above the starting balance.


The practical lesson: protect early profits aggressively. Do not give back more than 30–40% of any winning day's gains. The trailing floor will follow your best equity point.

Alert System

How to create a price alert in TradingView (step by step)
  • 1Right-click on any horizontal line you've drawn → select "Add Alert at [price]" from the context menu. OR press Alt + A on Windows / Option + A on Mac to open the alert creator.
  • 2In the alert dialog, set the Condition: use "Crossing" for a two-sided alert (fires on both break up and break down). Use "Crossing Up" for breakout alerts. Use "Crossing Down" for breakdown alerts.
  • 3Under Actions, enable: "Show popup window" (on-screen notification) and "Play sound" (audible alert). If you are away from the desk, also enable "Send email" to your registered TradingView email.
  • 4In the Alert name field, write a descriptive name (not just a price). Example: "MES PDH 5325.50 BREAK" is far more useful than "Alert #7" when it fires.
  • 5In the Message field, write your action reminder (see templates below). Click "Create." TradingView free accounts have a limited number of active alerts — prioritize the most critical levels.
  • 6Important: Alerts are attention triggers only — not automatic trade signals. When an alert fires, run your Pre-Trade Checklist before doing anything. An alert means "look here now," not "enter now."

08 — 30-Day Plan

Learning by Action
30-Day Plan

The most important rule of this 30-day plan: sequence matters more than speed. Every day spent on simulation instead of live money is a day your mistakes are free. The goal is not to make money this month — the goal is to build habits that will allow you to make money for years.
01
Chart School — No trading at all
Days 1–7 · Observe, learn, prepare
Observer
Day 1–2
Set up TradingView completely: all 4 symbols added, 3-chart layout configured, VWAP + 9 EMA + 21 EMA + Volume added. Watch the RTH session without doing anything. Just observe price movement and how it interacts with VWAP.
Day 3–4
Practice drawing PDH, PDL, ONH, ONL every morning before 9:30 AM. Set your 6 daily alerts. Study how price interacts with each level throughout the session. Do not think about trading — just watch and label what you see.
Day 5–7
Screenshot at least 10 setups per day that match the strategy (pullback to key level + VWAP alignment + confirmation candle). Label each screenshot: symbol, level, candle type, whether you would have taken it, and what you think would have happened. No live trading, no sim yet.
Week 1 Goal: Be able to identify key levels, trend direction, and VWAP relationship within 60 seconds of looking at any chart. Speed of recognition comes from repetition.
02
Simulator Trading — Paper trades only
Days 8–14 · Execute in simulation, journal everything
Simulator
Day 8–9
Enable Paper Trading mode on TradingView (toggle in the bottom toolbar). Trade MES and MNQ only. Use the exact position size you would use in real trading — do not use 100 contracts just because it's sim. Fake sizes create fake habits.
Day 10–11
Run the Pre-Trade Checklist for every single sim trade — even when you feel impatient. If a trade doesn't pass the checklist, skip it, even in simulation. The habit of checklist adherence is what you're building, not profits.
Day 12–14
Journal every trade the moment it closes (see Section 09 for template). Record entry, stop, target, result, and one specific lesson. Review your journal each evening. Identify your most repeated mistake by the end of the week.
Week 2 Goal: 5 days of positive sim P&L (any amount) while following all rules. If you are profitable but breaking rules — that's a failure. Rules adherence ranks above profits.
03
Consistency — Build the habit
Days 15–21 · Reduce mistakes, find your rhythm
Consistency
Day 15–17
Continue sim trading. Begin tracking metrics: win rate (%), average R:R on winning trades, number of checklist violations per day. If you had 3+ violations in Week 2, go back to Week 1 screenshot practice for 2 more days before continuing.
Day 18–19
Review your journal from Days 8–17. Write down your top 3 repeated mistakes (e.g., "entered before candle closed," "chased breakout," "moved stop wider"). For each mistake, write a specific rule to fix it. Add these to a personal addendum to your checklist.
Day 20–21
Aim for zero checklist violations for 3 consecutive days in a row. This is the Week 3 test. The result (profit or loss) matters less than clean execution. A clean losing day is better than a profitable day full of rule violations.
Week 3 Goal: 3 consecutive days with zero checklist violations. This demonstrates that consistent, disciplined execution is becoming automatic.
04
Prop Firm Readiness Test
Days 22–30 · Simulate a real challenge, make the go/no-go decision
Evaluation
Day 22–24
Set up a "mock challenge" in your sim account. Write down: daily loss limit, trailing drawdown limit, profit target, max trades per day, and challenge duration. Apply these rules identically to how a real prop firm would. Reset your sim account to the starting balance.
Day 25–27
Trade the mock challenge for 5 full simulated "challenge days." If you violate any rule on any day (daily loss limit hit, trailing drawdown breached, too many trades), note it — but continue. At the end, count total violations. The standard: 0–1 violations in 5 days is a pass.
Day 28–30
Review your complete 30-day journal. Calculate: overall win rate, average R:R ratio, biggest single mistake, biggest strength. Present it to yourself honestly. Only proceed to a real prop challenge if: win rate above 45%, average R:R above 1.3, fewer than 2 rule violations in Week 4.
Week 4 Goal: Pass your own mock challenge with no more than 1 rule violation. If you violated rules 2+ times in Week 4, repeat Week 3 and run another mock challenge before spending money on a real evaluation.

10 — Daily Card

Daily Trading Card
Print & Follow

This is your one-page daily operating card. Run through it every single morning before touching a chart. In the evening, review the "End of Day" section. Print it and keep it beside your keyboard.
Before you open TradingView
  • I have checked the economic calendar for today's high-impact USD events
  • I have written today's daily loss limit: I stop if I lose $___
  • I have written today's max trade count: I stop after ___ trades (max 3)
  • I am calm and in an appropriate mental state to trade
Morning chart preparation (complete by 9:25 AM ET)
  • Drew Previous Day High (PDH) on 15-min chart
  • Drew Previous Day Low (PDL) on 15-min chart
  • Drew Overnight High (ONH) on 15-min chart
  • Drew Overnight Low (ONL) on 15-min chart
  • Set all 6 daily price alerts (PDH, PDL, ONH, ONL, VWAP up/down)
  • Wrote my bias sentence: "Bias is ___ (LONG/SHORT/NEUTRAL) if price ___ [level]"
  • Confirmed VWAP indicator is active and reset at today's open
  • Confirmed simulator / brokerage connection is working
Before every trade — all 8 must be YES
  • Near a meaningful key level? (PDH, PDL, ONH, ONL, VWAP, OR)
  • Trend clear on 15-min? (Clean HH/HL or LH/LL — no tangled EMAs)
  • VWAP aligned? (Long above VWAP / Short below VWAP)
  • Risk/reward at least 1:1? (Target distance ÷ stop distance ≥ 1.0)
  • Stop placed at a logical structure? (Behind a level — not arbitrary)
  • Not chasing price? (Price is still near the setup level)
  • No major news event within 5 minutes? (Checked calendar)
  • Daily loss/trade limit not yet hit? (Still within today's rules)
Stop trading immediately if ANY of these are true
❌ Daily loss limit hit
❌ 2 consecutive losses
❌ 3 trades taken today
❌ Past 11:30 AM ET
❌ Feeling emotional (anger, fear, euphoria)
❌ Moved a stop against a trade
While in a trade — never do these
⚠ Move stop wider to avoid a loss
⚠ Add to a losing position
⚠ Enter another trade while in one
⚠ Watch P&L constantly (watch price only)
End of day — complete before closing TradingView
  • Journaled all trades with entry, stop, target, result, and one lesson each
  • Saved annotated screenshots of every trade (entry and exit marked)
  • Closed or confirmed all positions (no overnight holds as a beginner)
  • Deleted today's alerts — ready to redraw fresh levels tomorrow
  • Wrote one sentence: what I did well today / what to improve tomorrow
✓ Today's purpose: Follow rules perfectly. Profit is the result of consistent rule-following — not the goal of any individual day. A perfectly-executed losing day is a better result than a profitable day full of rule violations.
